FIRST DECISION — Is there learning signal here?

Many conversations are task completion (write this code, format this document, generate this content) or simple factual lookup with no evidence of the human building understanding, struggling with concepts, or changing how they think. If that's the case, write one sentence saying so and stop. Do not invent learning patterns from thin evidence.

A conversation HAS learning signal if the human is: building or revising a mental model, working through confusion, asking "why" or "how" questions that go beyond the immediate task, pushing back on explanations, or showing visible shifts in understanding. Even task-oriented conversations can contain learning signal — look for moments where the human is figuring something out, not just directing the AI.

IF THERE IS LEARNING SIGNAL, extract concrete findings. Each finding captures a key exchange paired with what it reveals and what a tutor should do. Use this format for each finding:

EXCHANGE: [the relevant portion of the conversation that shows the learning signal — include both AI and human turns when the signal depends on the interaction between them. Use exact quotes where possible, tight paraphrases marked with ~ when too long. For the most important findings, show the full back-and-forth that makes the signal legible on its own. For simpler findings, a single quote from either side is fine.]
SIGNAL: [what this reveals about how the learner thinks, learns, or gets stuck — one or two sentences]
TUTOR: [what a future tutor should do or avoid based on this — concrete and actionable]

Extract as many findings as the conversation supports. A short conversation with one interesting moment might yield 1-3 findings. A deep 50-message exchange might yield 8-15. Categories to look for:

- Learning approach: how the learner engages with new material (asks for examples? challenges claims? builds analogies? tests against own experience?)
- Sticking points: where the learner got stuck and what specifically they couldn't reconcile
- Breakthroughs: what was the learner's state before and after, and what caused the shift
- Incorrect models: assumptions the learner held that turned out wrong, and how they surfaced
- Pushback patterns: when the learner challenged the AI and whether it was productive
- What landed: explanations or framings that worked — evidenced by the learner's response
- What failed: approaches the AI tried that the learner ignored, rejected, or got more confused by
- Prior knowledge: what the learner clearly already knows (tells the tutor where NOT to start)

RULES:
- Do NOT narrate or summarize the conversation. No "the conversation started with X, then moved to Y." No retelling what happened. Only extracted findings.
- Every finding must include direct quotes or tight paraphrases as evidence. No unsupported claims.
- Include AI context in the EXCHANGE when the signal depends on it. For breakthroughs, show what the AI said that worked. For failures, show what the AI tried that didn't land. For pushback, show what the learner was responding to. A reader should understand the finding without reading the original conversation.
- Not every EXCHANGE needs to be a full back-and-forth. A single user quote is fine when it stands alone. Expand to multi-turn only when the interaction is what matters.
- The TUTOR line must be specific enough to act on. "Be patient" is useless. "Use dynamic thought experiments to arrive at static states — this learner's model of force is tied to motion, so static equilibrium needs a dynamic narrative to feel real" is useful.
- Do not invent patterns from thin evidence. If the conversation only supports 2 findings, write 2.
- Do not repeat the same signal in different words. Each finding should be distinct.
